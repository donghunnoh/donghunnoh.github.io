Surface Material Dataset for Robotics Applications (SMDRA): A Dataset with Friction Coefficient and RGB-D for Surface Segmentation

Publication
Project Overview
This research presents the Surface Material Dataset for Robotics Applications (SMDRA), a comprehensive dataset designed to facilitate computer vision research in robotics. The dataset stands out by providing a fusion of RGB color imagery, depth data, and unique pixel-wise friction coefficient measurements for ten different materials commonly found in the environment. This dataset aims to support the development and testing of robotic systems that interact physically with their surroundings, such as manipulators and mobile robots, by providing detailed surface characterization that is critical for tasks requiring tactile sensing and manipulation.
My Contributions
I was involved in the entire process, from developing the friction coefficient measurement device to training the neural networks with the dataset.
We introduced the Surface Material Dataset for Robotics Applications (SMDRA), an innovative collection aimed at enhancing computer vision in robotics. The dataset is unique for integrating RGB-D imagery with friction coefficient data across ten different materials, chosen for their prevalence in daily environments and relevance to robotics. This integration is crucial for developing robotic applications that necessitate sophisticated interaction with their surroundings, such as in tasks requiring precise manipulation and tactile feedback.

The depth data within SMDRA is captured using an active stereo camera, noted for its effectiveness in various robotic applications, including those in outdoor settings. The addition of friction coefficient data enriches the dataset, providing a dimension of physical interaction akin to the human sense of touch, which is vital for decision-making in robotics, from determining grasping strength and orientation to navigating unstructured environments. This multifaceted data allows for a more profound understanding and modeling of material properties, essential for physical interactions in robotics.

We utilized a novel friction measuring instrument to gather the dataset's friction data. We confirmed the viability of training established Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) architectures, such as the Fully Convolutional Network (FCN) and U-Net, using the SMDRA. The study demonstrates that SMDRA can be used to train neural network models effectively for segmentation tasks, highlighting that the dataset's diverse modalities provide valuable information that researchers can leverage to enhance robotic perception and interaction capabilities.

We're developing a neural network model to predict friction coefficients and segment surfaces using RGB-D data. Expanding our dataset, including different materials and conditions like wetness, is crucial for applying this model in real-world robotic scenarios. Our preliminary findings indicate some materials have higher friction coefficients when wet, which is unexpected and valuable for our model's accuracy. Future plans include testing on various robotic platforms, including quadruped robots for movement in diverse environments and robotic arms for improved grasping, to confirm the model's effectiveness.
